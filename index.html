Discoveries & Insights

Dataset Exploration

The main dataset analyzed in this exploration is housing_sf_other_w_census.csv. We can read the metadata in Metadata_housing_sf_other_w_census.txt to get a better understanding of what it contains:

Description: This dataset was created from MAPC's Zoning Atlas. It consists of spatial data and associated tabular data for the single-family and other types of housing in zoning districts of the 101 municipalities in the MAPC region. This dataset only keeps housing zones in Metro Boston and combines zones according to whether it is only single-family zoning by municipalities. Besides, this data aggregates the race and income data extracted from census data. There are two rows for some municipalities in this dataset to indicate only single-family zoning and other housing zoning.
Before diving into the spatial analysis, I performed a thorough profiling of the individual fields within the dataset.

1. Municipal Structure & Boolean Flags

The dataset contains 153 Muni IDs representing 101 distinct municipalities. My initial row count revealed that 52 municipalities appear twice, meaning they contain both "Only Single-Family" zoning and "Other" housing zones. This structural choice allows for a direct comparison of restrictive versus permissive zoning within the same town. To improve data clarity, I converted the "Only Single Family" and "MBTA Community" flags into boolean variables. Additionally, I noted that the "Is Housing" column was redundant, as the dataset is pre-filtered for residential land use.

2. Single Family Zoning

The % Single Family field represents the percentage of how much of residential land in that municipality is zoned exclusively for single-family homes. The values are equal to: (the land area of a single family for that municipality)/(land area of the housing zoning for that municipality). This field is useful for understanding the zoning policies of each municipality.

2. The Population Sample Paradox

A critical observation occurred when reviewing total population counts: high-density cities like Boston showed a population of only ~1,000 residents. This dataset provides a zoning-based sample rather than a full census. The captured population represents only those living in purely residential zones. Residents in "Complex Zones" (Mixed-Use or Special Districts) are excluded, meaning our findings reflect the demographics of the region's purely residential footprint rather than the entire urban fabric.

3. Investigating the Temporal Gap (2010 vs. 2018)

The dataset merges 2010 Census household types (fhh/nfhh) with 2014-2018 ACS income data. I investigated whether these totals added up correctly and discovered a significant temporal gap. The total household count in 2010 is given by the sum of the fhh and nfhh fields. The total household count in 2018 is given by the Hh field. We can compute the difference between the two as Hh - (fhh + nfhh), creating a new Household Temporal Difference field.

These figures show that there has been a significant change in household counts between 2010 and 2018, and highlights the nuances of using aggregated data from multiple sources. If we do a quick sanity check by comparing household to population counts, we should expect to see a positive linear correlation between the two. However, this is not the case for the 2018 household count: Thus, we should be careful when using the Hh field (2018 household count) to make inferences about the population, and vice versa.

Question 1: How does single-family zoning policy correlate with the presence of the student/young professional demographic, and where are the "tension points" where restrictive zoning may be forcing students and young professionals into precarious or "illegal" housing situations?

To approximate the population of students and young professionals, I developed a Roommate Lifestyle Index. This metric isolates residents aged 18-64 living in Non-Family Households (NFHH)s, which are proxy characteristics for this "Roommate Lifestyle" demographic. By filtering the data to the municipalities with "Other" housing zoning, I investigated how zoning policy dictates where this demographic is allowed to exist.

My initial hypothesis was that the municipalities with the most restrictive single-family zoning would have the highest roommate density, where the roommate population is squeezed into a small area. By contrast, I predicted that the municipalities with the most permissive single-family zoning would have the lowest roommate density, since there would be more land available for non-single-family housing.

Indeed, the results show that roommate density is highest in municipalities that have >90% single-family zoning. Notably, there is a spike in Sherborn, which has over 16x the density of the next highest municipality. Why is this the case? Looking back at the data, Sherborn has a total of 17 family households and 44 non-family households, with a high roommate lifestyle index of 0.25 and only 0.1315% of land available for non-single-family housing. These small numbers exaggerate the roommate density, as the denominator is so small. Intuitively, if Sherborn has zoned 99.86% of its land for single-family housing only, but has over 2.5x non-family households over family households, the density of roommates living in non-single-family housing would be quite high. However, the degree to which it is higher than other municipalities seems to be a function of its relative size. Thus, we need to be careful when interpreting the roommate density in Sherborn, and other municipalities with similar small numbers.

Overall, the results show that cities with more restrictive single-family zoning have more roommate density in their non-single-family housing zones. For the demographic of students and young professionals, this manifests in a competitive housing market for non-single-family housing. This may lead to them finding housing in overcrowded units, unpermitted basement suites, or informal living arrangements that bypass official zoning. When the legal "Other" housing supply is restricted to a fraction of a percent (as in the Sherborn example), the mismatch between the demographic reality and the legal code creates a regional pressure cooker, forcing the "Roommate" population into precarious or technically illegal housing situations.

Question 2: To what extent does "Exclusively Single-Family" zoning act as a legal "income floor" for residents, and is there a statistically significant correlation between these zones and the concentration of high-income households (>$200k) compared to "Other" housing zones?

To investigate this question, I analyzed the income data for the municipalities with "Other" housing zoning. I computed the percentage of households with income greater than $200,000 for each municipality, and compared it to the percentage of households with income greater than $200,000 for the municipalities with "Only Single-Family" zoning.

Within the same municipality, how does the income distribution compare between single-family and other housing zones? Does the restrictiveness of single-family zoning lead to a higher difference in income between the two zoning types? While individual municipalities may not have a strong correlation between single-family zoning and high-income households, the regional trend suggests that single-family zoning is a legal "income floor" for residents. This is consistent with the findings of the "Exclusionary by Design" report, which argues that zoning has historically been used as a tool for class and race exclusion.

Question 3: What is the quantitative potential of the new state-wide Accessory Dwelling Unit (ADU) mandate within exclusively single-family municipalities (like Newton or Quincy), and can a modest 5% adoption rate contribute meaningfully to the Affordable Homes Act's goal of 200,000 new units by 2030?

Notably, I proposed this research question before realizing the data was gathered from 2010-2018. Since then, the population and household counts have changed significantly, so any quantitative analysis would need to be updated to reflect the current data. However, we can still make some observations about the potential of ADUs assuming 2018 household counts. We can approximate the number of ADUs that could be built in each municipality by taking 5% of the total households in that municipality multiplied by the single-family zoning percentage.

By constructing ADUs only 5% of existing single-family zoning, we can build 2,880 new housing units. This is a modest contribution to the Affordable Homes Act's goal of 200,000 new units by 2030. However, we need to consider that the baseline of 2018 household counts is not representative of the current population and household counts, and that the 5% adoption rate is a conservative estimate. Building ADUs could help alleviate the housing shortage in these municipalities, providing a desirable and legal housing option for groups that are currently under high pressure from restrictive zoning policies (e.g. students and young professionals). This represents a realistic "middle path" for regional density that requires no new land acquisition or aggressive upzoning.

Summary

This investigation into the housing supply and zoning policies in Metro Boston reveals that the current housing crisis is not merely a product of market demand, but a deliberate outcome of spatial policy. Through exploratory data analysis of the MAPC Zoning Atlas and Census records, a few core lessons emerge:

  • Density is policy-driven, not just demographic: The "Roommate Squeeze" seen in towns with high single-family zoning shows that hyper-density often occurs not because a population chooses it, but because zoning mandates a "sliver" of available land for multi-family use. When we restrict land to 99% single-family use, we create artificial tension points that force the certain demographics, such as students and the workforce, into precarious housing situations.
  • Zoning acts as a structural wealth wall: As an overall trend, single-family zoning serves as an effective economic filter. By legalizing only the most expensive form of housing (single-detached homes) on the majority of regional land, municipalities maintain high "prices of entry," successfully separating the wealthiest residents from the broader regional workforce. Although this pattern occurs on an overall level, it is not uniform across all municipalities.
  • The value of strategic incrementalism: While the potential for 2,880 ADU units may seem modest compared to state goals, these units are strategically significant. They represent a form of gentle upzoning, providing legal pathways for diversity in the region's most exclusive suburbs.
  • Data literacy requires context: Finally, this analysis highlights the importance of data provenance. Understanding the "Boston Discrepancy" (zoning-based sampling vs. full census) and the temporal gaps between 2010 and 2018 data was essential to avoid misleading conclusions about regional growth and density.
